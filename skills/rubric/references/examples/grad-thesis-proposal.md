# Rubric: Graduate Thesis Proposal

**Assignment:** Submit a 15-25 page thesis proposal outlining your research question, situating it in the scholarly literature, describing your proposed methodology, and justifying the contribution to your field. Follow your program's style guide (APA / Chicago / discipline-specific).

**Alignment:** Narrative holistic · 4 criteria x 4 levels · qualitative · no taxonomic tags (grad-level work is program-competency aligned)

## 1. Originality & Significance

**Does not yet meet.** The proposed question restates an existing study or is answered by readily-available literature. The significance is asserted but not argued. The reader finishes the section unclear on why this project should be undertaken or what gap it addresses.

**Approaching.** The question is distinct from prior work but the distinction is marginal or unclear. Significance is argued but the argument rests on general claims ("this topic is important") rather than specific gaps or debates. Some articulation of scholarly contribution is present but underdeveloped.

**Meets.** The question is clearly original and addresses a specific gap, tension, or debate in the literature. Significance is argued with reference to named scholars, schools of thought, or unresolved problems. A reader in the discipline can identify the contribution and why it matters.

**Exceeds.** The question reframes an existing debate or opens a new line of inquiry. Significance is argued across multiple dimensions — scholarly, methodological, and (where relevant) practical or political. A reader outside the immediate subfield can understand the stakes.

## 2. Literature Engagement

**Does not yet meet.** Literature review is a list of summaries with no synthesis. Key scholars in the subfield are missing or misattributed. Citations are inconsistent or missing.

**Approaching.** Literature review groups sources thematically but does not yet create a conversation among them. Most key scholars are included but some important voices are absent. Citations are consistent.

**Meets.** Literature review synthesizes sources into a coherent narrative about the state of the field. Scholars are in dialogue with one another; debates and points of agreement are identified. Coverage is broad and includes recent work (last 5 years) as well as foundational texts.

**Exceeds.** Literature review is a critical intervention — the candidate identifies patterns, tensions, or absences that others have not articulated. Engagement spans subfields and includes adjacent disciplines where relevant. The review positions the candidate as a participant in the scholarly conversation, not an observer.

## 3. Methodology

**Does not yet meet.** Methods section names an approach without justifying it. No explicit link between the research question and the chosen methods. Data sources or analytical procedures are vague or missing. Limitations unacknowledged.

**Approaching.** Methods section describes the approach and its implementation in reasonable detail. Some justification is provided but alternative methods are not considered. Limitations are acknowledged but not analyzed.

**Meets.** Methods section justifies the chosen approach relative to the research question, considers at least one alternative and explains why it was rejected, and describes data sources, sampling strategy (where applicable), and analytical procedures in enough detail for another researcher to evaluate feasibility. Limitations and mitigations are discussed.

**Exceeds.** Methods section demonstrates sophisticated methodological awareness — situates the approach within its tradition, engages with methodological debates relevant to the question, and anticipates challenges with concrete mitigation strategies. Pilot data or preliminary work is included where appropriate. Ethics, positionality, and reflexivity are addressed where relevant to the discipline.

## 4. Scholarly Communication

**Does not yet meet.** Writing is unclear at the paragraph level; key terms used inconsistently. Citation style violations throughout. Structure is linear but does not signal argument progression.

**Approaching.** Writing is generally clear but uneven. Key terms mostly consistent. Citation style largely followed with occasional errors. Structure is clear but transitions between sections are abrupt.

**Meets.** Writing is clear, precise, and appropriate to the discipline. Key terms are defined on first use and used consistently. Citation style flawlessly followed. Structure signals the argument — sections and subsections reinforce the progression from question to significance to method to anticipated contribution.

**Exceeds.** Writing is publication-quality — concise, precise, and rhetorically aware. Key terms are defined AND situated within their scholarly usage. Structure is not merely clear but cumulative, each section building on the last. The proposal reads as the work of an emerging scholar.

**Notes for committee:** No points are assigned deliberately — grad-level work is evaluated narratively. A proposal that "Meets" across all four criteria passes; any "Does not yet meet" triggers a revise-and-resubmit. For interdisciplinary programs, substitute relevant program competencies for the four criteria above.
