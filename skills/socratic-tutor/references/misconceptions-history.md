# History Misconceptions

Each entry: **Name → Student belief → Why wrong → Probe question.**

## Presentism
- **Belief:** Historical actors should have known what we now know; judging them by today's values tells the whole story.
- **Why wrong:** Context matters. Understanding doesn't require excusing, but it does require reconstructing what was knowable at the time.
- **Probe:** "What information did the people making this decision have? What would you have known if you lived then?"

## Great-man theory
- **Belief:** History is driven by a handful of famous individuals.
- **Why wrong:** Structural forces (economics, geography, disease, technology, institutions) shape what's possible. Individuals matter, but within systems.
- **Probe:** "If this person had never been born, would this outcome have been impossible? Or would someone else have done something similar? What does that tell you?"

## Monocausal explanations
- **Belief:** "World War I was caused by the assassination of Franz Ferdinand."
- **Why wrong:** Major events usually have many interlocking causes (structural + immediate + contingent).
- **Probe:** "If the assassination happened in 1850, would a world war have followed? What else had to be true for this spark to ignite the fire?"

## The past was simpler / more uniform
- **Belief:** "People in medieval Europe all believed X."
- **Why wrong:** Any large society has internal diversity, dissent, and change over time.
- **Probe:** "Does everyone in *our* society believe the same thing about Y? Why assume a past society was more uniform than ours?"

## Progress is linear and inevitable
- **Belief:** Societies move steadily forward; things always get better.
- **Why wrong:** Regressions, collapses, and reversals are common. Progress on one axis often coexists with decline on another.
- **Probe:** "Name one thing that was better in 1900 than now. Now name one thing that's better now. What does that do to the 'things always improve' story?"

## Primary sources are neutral windows onto the past
- **Belief:** A letter, diary, or chronicle tells you what really happened.
- **Why wrong:** Every source has a perspective, audience, and purpose. 'What really happened' requires triangulation.
- **Probe:** "Who wrote this source? Who were they writing *to*? What would they have wanted their reader to believe?"

## History repeats exactly
- **Belief:** "This is exactly like [previous event]."
- **Why wrong:** Analogies illuminate but often distort. Structural similarities coexist with critical differences.
- **Probe:** "You said this is like [X]. What's one important way it's *not* like [X]? Does that change what we should predict?"
